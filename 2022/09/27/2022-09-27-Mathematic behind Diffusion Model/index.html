Mathematic behind Diffusion Model

This is a note for Diffusion Model Explanation Video by youtuber Outlier(09:33-24:21)

Reference:

Diffusion Models 模型的数学解释

1. Basic Intuition

The diffusion model gradually add noise to a given image and try to recover that image in a reversed progress through a neural network.

In general the model could simply take a completely noised image to generate original image by single step, but practice has proved it to be ineffective. Step-by-step strategy has been proved to be a more realistic and effective approach.

Here step-by-step strategy means the identical model takes a noised image and outputs a slightly de-noised image. Approaches to recover image from a noised one can be categorised into 3 classes:

  1. recover the whole image in one step

  2. recover a slightly de-noised image step by step

  3. predict the noise component in image step by step

Substantially, approach 2 and approach 3 have the same effect, for once noise component is calculated, one can recover a de-noised image by subtract noise component from input.

2. Basic Notation

  • Image

Let \(x_t\) be the image that being perturbed for \(t\) times. So \(x_0\) is the original image and \(x_T\) is the image when all perturbing procedure is done, which follows a isotropic Gaussian distribution. \(T\) is different across different papers. Initially it is set to \(1000\).

  • Forward Process

Let \(q\) be a function that describe the perturbing procedure step. It takes an image \(x_t\) as input and output an image \(x_{t+1}\) with more noise.

\[ q(x_t|x_{t-1}) \]

  • Backward Process

Similarly, Let \(p\) be a de-nosing function, which outputs a de-noised image \(x_{t-1}\) based on image \(x_{t}\). This part will be done through deep learning models.

\[ p(x_{t-1}|x_t) \]

3. Forward and Backward Process Deduction

By assuming that noise is subjected to Gaussian distribution, we have:

\[ q(x_t|x_{t-1})=\mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_tI) \tag{1} \]

where \(\mathcal{N}\) denotes as normal distribution. Output is denoted as \(x_{t-1}\). \(\beta_t\) is a coefficient that controls mean and variance of the distribution in each step. It is pre-set in accordance with step \(t\).

In favour of formula deduction, we define two new coefficients \(\alpha_t\) and \(\overline{\alpha_t}\) as follow:

\[ \alpha_t=1-\beta_t\\ \overline{\alpha_t}=\prod_{i=1}^t{\alpha_i} \]

We use \((1)\) to expand \(x_{t-1}\) term in equation \((1)\):

\[ \begin{aligned} q(x_t|x_{t-1})&=\mathcal{N}(x_t; \sqrt{1-\beta_t} x_{t-1}, \beta_tI)\\ &= \sqrt{1-\beta_t}x_{t-1}+\sqrt{\beta_t}\epsilon\\ &= \sqrt{1-\beta_t}(\sqrt{1-\beta_{t-1}}x_{t-2}+\sqrt{\beta_{t-1}}\epsilon)+\sqrt{\beta_t}\epsilon\\ &= \sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}}x_{t-2}+\sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}}\epsilon)+\sqrt{1-\alpha_{t}}\epsilon\\ &= \sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}\alpha_t}x_{t-2}+(\sqrt{\alpha_t-\alpha_{t-1}\alpha_t}+\sqrt{1-\alpha_t})\epsilon \end{aligned}\tag{2} \]

Here, we notice that two normal distribution is added. For normal distribution addition, we have:

\[ \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \delta_0^2)+\mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \delta_1^2)=\mathcal{N}(\mu_0+\mu_1, \delta_0^2,+\delta_1^2) \]

So \((2)\) equals:

\[ \begin{aligned} x_t=q(x_t|x_{t-1})&=\sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}\alpha_t}x_{t-2}+(\sqrt{\alpha_t-\alpha_{t-1}\alpha_t}+\sqrt{1-\alpha_t})\epsilon\\ &= \sqrt{\alpha_{t-1}\alpha_t}x_{t-2}+\sqrt{1-\alpha_{t-1}\alpha_t}\epsilon\\ & \text{recursive substitution}\\ &= \sqrt{\prod_{i=1}^t\alpha_i}x_0+\sqrt{1-\prod_{i=1}^t\alpha_i}\epsilon\\ &= \sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}x_0+\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha_t}}\epsilon \end{aligned}\tag{2} \]

Likewise, \(p\) can be defined as:

\[ p(x_{t-1}|x_t)=\mathcal{N}(x_{x-1}; \mu_\theta(x_t,t),\Sigma_\theta(x_t,t)) \]

where \(\mu_{\theta}\) is a neural network that predict mean, \(\Sigma_\theta\) can be seen as a fixed parameter according to training schedule.

4. Loss Function

Bear in mind that our final goal is to predict the noise component in each step.

We use negative log likelihood as loss function to maximise the probability to generate real image. \[ \text{Loss Function} = -log(p_{\theta}(x_0)) \] The probability of \(p_\theta(x_0)\) is hard to calculate, since it is related with \(x_0, x_1, \cdots, x_T\). We have the following inequation instead. \[ \text{Loss Function} = -log(p_\theta(x_0)) \leq -log(p_\theta(x_0)) + D_{KL}(q(x_{1:T}|x_0)||p_\theta(x_{1:T}|x_0)) \]

It is our goal to minimise the KL diversion between \(q\) and \(p_\theta\) since this diversion depict the distance between results of two progresses. Once the goal is reached, the KL diversion is \(0\).

We expand KL diversion into \(log\) form. \[ \begin{aligned} D_{KL}(q(x_{1: T}|x_{0}) || p_{\theta}(x_{1: T}|x_{0}))&=log \frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{p_{\theta}(x_{1: T} | x_{0})}\\ &=log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{\frac{p_{\theta}(x_{0} x_{1: T}) p_{\theta}(x_{1: T})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0})}}\\ &=log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{\frac{p_\theta(x_0,x_{1:T})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0})}}\\ &=log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{\frac{p_\theta(x_{0:T})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0})}}\\ &=log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0: T})}+log (p_{\theta}(x_{0})) \end{aligned} \] By expanding KL diversion term, the uncalculatable term is gone. The loss function remains as follow. \[ \text{Loss Function} \leq log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0: T})}\tag{3} \] The conditional probability can be expanded into serial product term as follow. \[ \begin{aligned} log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0: T})}&=log\frac{\prod_{i=1}^T{q(x_{i}|x_{i-1})}}{p_\theta(x_T)\prod_{i=1}^T{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}}\\ &=log\frac{\prod_{i=1}^T{q(x_{i}|x_{i-1})}}{\prod_{i=1}^T{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=1}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{i}|x_{i-1})}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{i}|x_{i-1})}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t)q(x_t)}{q(x_{t-1})}}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ \end{aligned}\tag{4} \] Focus on the first term in this equation, those probability with \(q(x_{t-1}|x_{t})\) is with high variance, since its real meaning is that to generate \(t-1\) from \(t\) and when t is large, the image is nearly completely noised. It is highly impossible to recover image correctly. A better way to deal with this issue is let it also condition on \(x_0\). \[ \begin{aligned} log\frac{q(x_{1: T}|x_{0})}{p_{\theta}(x_{0: T})}&=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t)q(x_t)}{q(x_{t-1})}}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &\Rightarrow\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)q(x_t|x_0)}{q(x_{t-1}|x_0)}}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)q(x_t|x_0)}{q(x_{t-1}|x_0)p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+\sum_{i=2}^T{log(\frac{q(x_t|x_0)}{q(x_{t-1}|x_0)}})+\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})}-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ & \text{expand term 2 would cancel many term}\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+log(\frac{q(x_T|x_0)}{q(x_1|x_0)})+log(\frac{q(x_{1}|x_{0})}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})})-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+log(\frac{q(x_T|x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1})})-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+log(q(x_T|x_0))-log(p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1}))-log(p_\theta(x_T))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+log(\frac{q(x_T|x_0)}{p_\theta(x_T)})-log(p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1}))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(log(\frac{q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)}{p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})}))+D_{KL}(q(x_T|x_0)||p_\theta(x_T))-log(p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1}))\\ &=\sum_{i=2}^T(D_{KL}(q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)||p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})))+D_{KL}(q(x_T|x_0)||p_\theta(x_T))-log(p_\theta(x_{0}|x_{1}))\\ \end{aligned} \] The second term would approach to \(0\) since it is the final goal to make \(q\) fully recover the original image.

We then closely exam the first term: \[ \sum_{i=2}^T(D_{KL}(q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)||p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i}))) \] It depict distance between \(p_\theta\) and \(q\).

\(p_\theta\) can be re-written into normal distribution form. Since we have the variance fixed in accordance with training schedule, we do not have to use model \(\theta\) to predict it. \[ p_\theta(x_{i-1}|x_{i})=\mathcal{N}(x_{i-1};\mu_\theta(x_t, t), \delta)=\mathcal{N}(x_{i-1};\mu_\theta(x_t, t), \beta I) \] \(q\) can also be re-written into normal distribution form. \[ q(x_{t-1}|x_t,x_0)=\mathcal{N}(x_{t-1};\tilde{\mu}(x_t,x_0), \tilde{\beta}I) \] Here, the coefficients \(\tilde{\mu}(x_t,x_0), \tilde{\beta}\) are scheduled as follow. Similarly, \(\beta\) is fixed. \[ \begin{aligned} \tilde{\mu}(x_t,x_0)&=\frac{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{t}+\frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_{t}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{0}\\ \tilde{\beta}_{t}&=\frac{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} \cdot \beta_{t} \end{aligned} \] For \(\tilde{\mu}\), we substitute \(x_0\) with \((2)\) in \(x_0\), then we have: \[ \begin{aligned} x_t&=\sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}x_0+\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha_t}}\epsilon\\ \Rightarrow x_0&=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}}(x_t-\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha_t}}\epsilon)\\ \tilde{\mu}(x_t,x_0)&=\frac{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{t}+\frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_{t}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{0}\\ &=\frac{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{t}+\frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_{t}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}}(x_t-\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha_t}}\epsilon)\\ &=\frac{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{t}+\frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_{t}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}}x_t-\frac{1}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}}\frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_{t}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha_t}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}} x_{t}+\frac{\beta_{t}}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}}x_t-\frac{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}} \beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\overline{\alpha_t}}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{1}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}(\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t-1})+\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}})x_t-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{1}{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}(\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}-\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}\overline{\alpha}_{t-1}+\frac{1-\alpha_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}})x_t-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t})}(\alpha_{t}-\overline{\alpha}_{t}+1-\alpha_{t})x_t-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t})}(1-\overline{\alpha}_{t})x_t-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}}x_t-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{\alpha_t}\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}}\epsilon\\ &=\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}}(x_t-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}})\epsilon \end{aligned} \] We use a MSE Loss to measure and minimise the distance of predict \(\mu_\theta\) and actual \(\tilde\mu\) \[ L_{t}=\frac{1}{2 \sigma_{t}^{2}}\left\|\tilde{\mu}_{t}\left(x_{t}, x_{0}\right)-\mu_{\theta}\left(x_{t}, t\right)\right\|^{2}=\frac{1}{2 \sigma_{t}^{2}}\left\|\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}}\left(x_{t}-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}} \epsilon\right)-\mu_{\theta}\left(x_{t}, t\right)\right\|^{2} \] In \(\mu\) there are two variable except for coefficients, in which \(x_t\) is input and \(\epsilon\) is noise component. We r]-write \(\mu_\theta\) in similar form so that we can focus more on predicting noise. \[ \begin{aligned} L_t&=\frac{1}{2 \sigma_{t}^{2}}\left\|\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}}\left(x_{t}-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}} \epsilon\right)-\frac{1}{\sqrt{\alpha_{t}}}\left(x_{t}-\frac{\beta_{t}}{\sqrt{1-\overline{\alpha}_{t}}} \epsilon_{\theta}\left(x_{t}, t\right)\right)\right\|^{2}\\ &=\frac{\beta_{t}^{2}}{2 \sigma_{t}^{2} \alpha_{t}\left(1-\hat{\alpha}_{t}\right)} \| \epsilon-\epsilon_{\theta}\left(x_{t}, t\right) \|^{2} \end{aligned} \] The coefficient before \(L2\) distance can be simply neglected since it would not affect training.

Get back to real loss function we were talking about. Now it is equivalent to \[ L_{VLB}=\sum_{t=2}^{T} D_{K L}(q(x_{t-1} | x_{t}, x_{0}) || p_{\theta}(x_{t-1} | x_{t}))-\log (p_{\theta}(x_{0} | x_{1}))\sim\|\epsilon-\epsilon_{\theta}(x_{t}, t)\|^{2}-\log (p_{\theta}(x_{0} | x_{1})) \] The last term can also be neglected and use a network to predict. Reason behind it won't be detailed in this article. The reader is refereed to origin video and paper to find more information.

The final loss is \[ L=\|\epsilon-\epsilon_{\theta}(x_{t}, t)\|^{2} \]
